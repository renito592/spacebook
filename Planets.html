The smallest planet in our solar system and nearest to the Sun, Mercury is only slightly larger than Earth's Moon.

Second planet from the Sun and our closest planetary neighbor, Venus is similar in structure and size to Earth, but it is now a very different world.

Our home planet is the third planet from the Sun, and the only place we know of so far that’s inhabited by living things.

The fourth planet from the Sun, Mars is a dusty, cold, desert world with a very thin atmosphere.

Fifth in line from the Sun, Jupiter is, by far, the largest planet in the solar system – more than twice as massive as all the other planets combined.

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest planet in our solar system.

The first planet found with the aid of a telescope, Uranus was discovered in 1781 by astronomer William Herschel, although he originally thought it was either a comet or a star.

Dark, cold and whipped by supersonic winds, ice giant Neptune is the eighth and most distant planet in our solar system.

Pluto—which is smaller than Earth’s Moon—has a heart-shaped glacier that’s the size of Texas and Oklahoma.

Dwarf planet Ceres is the largest object in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter and the only dwarf planet located in the inner solar system.

Makemake is located in the Kuiper Belt, a region outside the orbit of Neptune. Slightly smaller than Pluto, Makemake is the second-brightest object in the Kuiper Belt as seen from Earth.

Haumea is one of the fastest rotating large objects in our solar system. Its fast spin distorts Haumea's shape, making this dwarf planet look like a football.

Eris is one of the largest known dwarf planets in our solar system. It's about the same size as Pluto, but is three times farther from the Sun.

Sedna is a minor planet, or planetoid, that lies beyond the Kuiper Belt. Its home reaches to the far edges of the solar system, making it one of the most distant space bodies ever identified.
